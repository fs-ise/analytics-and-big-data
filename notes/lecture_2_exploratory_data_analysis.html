Learning Objectives

After this session, students should be able to:

  • Understand the purpose of exploratory data analysis (EDA)
  • Describe and interpret key characteristics of business data
  • Identify relationships and patterns in multivariate data
  • Use visualizations to explore and communicate insights
  • Understand the basic idea of the grammar of graphics in Python (Plotly)

Badly formatted data blocks analysis — data preparation unlocks it.

Data preparation:

  • Statistics traditionally relies on a controlled mode of data collection
  • Repurposing of data, analyzing transactional data is not a typical setting
  • Data quality measures are relatively limited (focused on outliers)
  • Data preparation (manual correction or the use of synthetic data) are even discarded as a questionable research practice

Metaphor: statisticians carefully collect their data on their own (live on their own island) - business analysts have to work with existing data (which can be very messy)

But: Sometimes it is better to know/predict something even if we cannot explain it instead of doing nothing! Examples:

  • Recommendation systems: Which movie are you likely to watch next? thousands of variables may be available; we may never know why exactly a particular recommendation works, but recommendations generally improve engagement/revenue. (“We don’t know/don’t give a recommendation” is the worst option) -> Business decision to recommend anything is always better than no recommendation at all
  • Fraud detection: prediction of irregular and fraudulent transactions can be very accurate, but the attackers goal is to behave in a way that is hard to anticipate. Prediction may focus on identifying cases that are “not behaving regularly” (rather than identifying generalizable patterns)

0. Why Exploratory Data Analysis?

  • What is EDA and why it matters in business decision-making
  • EDA vs. confirmatory analysis (hypotheses, models)
  • Typical business use cases:
    • Understanding customers
    • Exploring sales and performance data
    • Detecting anomalies and outliers
  • EDA as an iterative and question-driven process

1. Univariate Exploratory Data Analysis

“What’s in my data?”

1.1 Types of Variables

  • Numerical (continuous, discrete)
  • Categorical (nominal, ordinal)
  • Why variable types matter for analysis and visualization

1.2 Descriptive Statistics

  • Central tendency: mean, median, mode
  • Dispersion: range, variance, standard deviation
  • Quantiles and interquartile range (IQR)
  • Interpreting statistics in a business context

1.3 Distribution Analysis

  • Histograms and density plots
  • Boxplots and outliers
  • Skewness and heavy tails
  • Typical business examples:
    • Income and revenue distributions
    • Customer activity and usage data

1.4 Categorical Variables

  • Frequency tables
  • Relative frequencies and percentages
  • Bar charts
  • Business questions:
    • Which customer segment is largest?
    • How are products distributed across categories?

2. Multivariate Exploratory Data Analysis

“How do variables relate to each other?”

2.1 Relationships Between Numerical Variables

  • Scatterplots
  • Correlation (Pearson, Spearman – intuition only)
  • Interpreting correlation (and common pitfalls)
  • Business examples:
    • Price and demand
    • Marketing spend and sales

2.2 Numerical and Categorical Variables

  • Grouped summaries (means by category)
  • Boxplots by group
  • Comparing distributions across segments
  • Business examples:
    • Revenue by customer segment
    • Satisfaction scores by region

2.3 Relationships Between Categorical Variables

  • Contingency tables
  • Percentages and conditional distributions
  • Simple association analysis
  • Business examples:
    • Product category × return behavior
    • Channel × conversion outcome

2.4 From Patterns to Questions

  • EDA does not “prove” causality
  • Using EDA to generate hypotheses
  • Deciding what to analyze next

3. Visual Exploratory Data Analysis

“Seeing and communicating patterns”

3.1 Why Visualization Matters

  • Human pattern recognition
  • Visualization for exploration vs. presentation
  • Common visualization mistakes in business reports

3.2 Choosing the Right Chart

  • When to use:
    • Bar charts
    • Line charts
    • Scatterplots
    • Boxplots
  • Matching chart types to questions and data types

3.3 Grammar of Graphics (Conceptual)

  • Data
  • Aesthetics (x, y, color, size)
  • Geometries (points, lines, bars)
  • Facets (small multiples)
  • Why this idea scales well for complex analyses

3.4 Visual EDA in Python with Plotly

  • Why Plotly for business analytics:
    • Interactivity
    • Easy exploration of subgroups
  • Example visual analyses:
    • Scatterplot with color by category
    • Distribution plots by segment
    • Interactive filtering and hover information

4. Putting It All Together

  • EDA as an iterative workflow:
    1. Summarize variables
    2. Explore relationships
    3. Visualize and refine questions
  • From raw data to business insight
  • Limitations of EDA
  • Transition to:
    • Statistical modeling
    • Machine learning
    • Dashboards and reporting

Key Takeaways

  • EDA helps you understand data before modeling it
  • Start simple: univariate → multivariate → visual
  • Focus on business questions, not just techniques
  • Visualization is a core analytical skill, not just decoration
